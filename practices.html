Writing Papers

Planning

When is your paper due? Are you currently frantically writing for a deadline? The next time you start to write a paper, consider using a paper writing plan with multiple phases of iteration to relieve some of the pressure.

Structure of an Introduction

There is no one true way to write the Introduction of a paper. This structure outlined here is based on Sharon Goldwater's notes on Writing Research Papers.

  • Situate: What is the overall context of the work? Is there an overarching theoretical or practical context?
  • Open problem: What is the specific open problem studied in this paper? Why is it important?
  • Approach: Briefly, how do we approach the open problem? Is there any motivation for this approach?
  • Findings: If we conducted any experiments, what are the main findings? Why are these findings important?

Problem Formulations

A well-written Problem Formulation makes it easier for the reader to understand the task we are working on, and any assumptions about the data and evaluation. I learned about the importance of this section from Mirella Lapata (Example 1) and used it in Section 2 of the IJCNLP paper (Example 2) but there are other examples, such as Section 2.1 (Example 3) or Section 3 — Section 3.1 (Example 4).

Wording

Sometimes it can be difficult to find the words to express yourself. The Academic Phrasebank is a useful resource and inspiration for how to write academic-sounding sentences, but be careful to avoid writing in cliched academese.

It is tempting to use complicated words to make your work sound impressive. Use good words instead!

Reviewing Papers

Reviewing papers is an important part of our job and we should remember that it’s a voluntary service that we do for others and they do for us too. I think it is optimal to review papers on topics where you believe you have some expertise because you can quickly identify the main contributions of the work. How should you go about review papers? That’s tricky but there is some useful guidance out there. The ACL provides a reviewing tutorial to help you understand the role and goal of peer review. There was also a CVPR workshop on being a good citizen of the community.

Author Response

Many conferences and journals allow authors to respond to their reviews to correct technical misunderstandings or to provide additional results and analyses. First step: take a deep breath. There are often misunderstandings between what you wrote and what the reader understood. Second step: take a look at Devi Parikh's How we write rebuttals. Third: we use a review response template to prepare our rebuttal in collaboration with all the authors.

Talks

Giving talks is a crucial part of our work because we are communicating our research and findings to other people. We aim for the following with our talks:

  • Have a clear message;
  • Present a self-contained story about the contribution of our work; and
  • Engage the audience.

We usually practice our talks in-advance and incorporate feedback from the rehearsal audience.

You should ask other people for advice on how to structure your talk. It usually takes several iterations to get it right.

Also check out Sharon Goldwater's advice on conference presentations.
