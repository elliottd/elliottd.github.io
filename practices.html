Writing Papers

Structure of an Introduction

There is no one true way to write the Introduction of a paper. This structure outlined here is based on Sharon Goldwater's notes on Writing Research Papers.

  • Situate: What is the overall context of the work? Is there an overarching theoretical or practical context?
  • Open problem: What is the specific open problem studied in this paper? Why is it important?
  • Approach: Briefly, how do we approach the open problem? Is there any motivation for this approach?
  • Findings: If we conducted any experiments, what are the main findings? Why are these findings important?

Wording

Sometimes it can be difficult to find the words to express yourself. The Academic Phrasebank is a useful resource and inspiration for how to write academic-sounding sentences, but be careful to avoid writing in cliched academese.
